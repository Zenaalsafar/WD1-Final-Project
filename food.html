Kurdish Cuisine

Since I moved out of Kurdistan, beside missing my family, friends, and nature in Kurdistan Bashur, I miss the food the most. I miss the taste of vegetables and fruits back home. I miss the meals and the family gatehring around the table. I miss breakfasts, lunches, dinners, and desserts. I miss the smell of fresh bread and the gathering around the table that are not so common in this part of the world where I live today. I am getting hungery writing this.

Let me take you on a food tour. This might be similar to food in neighbouring countries, but each have their own way of adding spice which makes it different. First things first, breakfast. We in Kurdistan are not used to eating cereal and milk for breakfast. This trend is relatively new and only practiced by very few individuals and mostly frowned upon. A typical Kurdish breakfast usually consiste of eggs, cheese, yogurt, cream, honey, walnuts, beans, warm bread, and black tea. The combination varies depending on the region. I for one never had beans for breakfast until I was 28 years old when I went out for breakfast with friends at a resturant in old town in Erbil, Kurdistan. It is true that each household will have their own combination of the items mentioned above, however, there is never just one item and there is always tea. Cuisine

Now let's talk about the Kurdish cuisines. There is a big difference between everyday food in Kurdish household and occasion cuisine or holidays. Daily kurdish food consist of mainly rice, a soup, beef or chicken (if affordable), and salad. This is called Brinj o Afik/Brinj u Shla The most common type of rice is kurish rice, which has a slightly smaller size compared to that of basmati rice and has a richer flavour—I never liked it, but majority of the Kurds love it. The most common type of soup is beans soup with beef or beef broth. The most common type of salad is that of cucummber and tomates with pomegranate sauce.

Biryani

Kurds have many cuisine that you see made suring the weekends, when guests are around, and in special occasions. These can vary. I would say the most popular and common foods are Biryani, Yaprax/Dolma, Tershik/Kifta, and Shifta. Biryani is not a new food nor special to only Kurds, but it is a popular easy to make delicious food. In kurdistan, Biryani is usually made by mixing rice with fried almonds, chicken, raisens (unfortunetly), and some mis potatos, carrots, and peas. See the picture to the right of this paraghraph. Dolma

Kurdish Dolma is basically vegtables stuffed with rice and beef. It sounds very easy when I put it like this, but this is a time consuming cuisine. You would need to prepare various vegetables by boiling, cutting, and removing the insides for them to be stuffed. Then you need to cook the rice and the beef with paresley, garlice, tomato puree, and spices. Kuridish people like the dolma to be spicy and sour, therefore, there is a lot of lemon and chilli involved. If you want to make Kurdish Dolma, here is a short video on tiktok of how to make it.

Kifta

Turn to talk about my favourate food, Kifta or as we call it Tirshik. This dish is the only dish I would ever ask for whenever I am going back home or when anyone asks me what I want them to cook for me. It is the best and it is also time consuming. It is basically dumpling soup if you want to see it in simple terms. It is made by stuffing jarish with with beef that has been fried with onions, parsely, and spices. Then boiling them in a tomato and vegetable soup, depending on the household different vegetables are added int eh soup. If you want to learn how to make kifta, here is short Youtube video of how to make kifta.

Desert

All of this and we have not talked about the sweets. In kurdistan, cake is not a typical desert. Usually a desert is some baked pasteries such as Kada/Kulecha or some of the more internationally famous deserts like Baklava and Kunafa. Kada are made in every Kurdish and Iraqi household at least twice a year during Eid Al Fitr and Eid Al Adha. My favourate are the ones stuffed with dates. They are usually stuffed with grated cocunut, walnuts, and sesames.

Finally, Baklava. Since this one doesn't need introduction. It is available everywhere in the world and has various types that have unique looks and flavours. I won't say much more and just share a picture.
